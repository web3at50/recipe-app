## Background: What is Lupin?

Lupin (also spelled "lupine") is a legume from the same family as peanuts, soybeans, and other beans. The seeds of the lupin plant are crushed to make lupin flour, which is used in various food products.

**Key Facts:**
- **One of the 14 major allergens** regulated in the UK (must be declared on labels)
- **More common in Europe** (especially France, Italy, and Mediterranean countries) than in the UK
- **Used as a gluten-free alternative** - increasingly found in gluten-free products
- **High in protein** - used in vegan and vegetarian products
- **Cross-reactivity with peanuts** - 5-37% of people with peanut allergy may also react to lupin
- **Rising prevalence** - In France, lupin became the 4th most frequent cause of severe food anaphylaxis by 2002

**Types of Reactions:**
- IgE-mediated food allergy (immediate allergic reaction)
- Can cause severe anaphylaxis in susceptible individuals
- Cross-reactions possible in those with peanut allergy (both are legumes)

For this app, treat lupin allergy as requiring strict lupin avoidance, and always flag the peanut connection.

## ‚ö†Ô∏è TRICKY CASES - Important Notes

### Peanut Allergy Connection - CRITICAL
**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH RISK if you have peanut allergy
**Explanation:** Lupin is a legume in the same family as peanuts. Studies show 5-37% of people with peanut allergy also react to lupin due to similar proteins (cross-reactivity).
**Allergen proteins:** Lupin proteins are structurally similar to peanut proteins (Ara h 1, Ara h 3, Ara h 8).
**Important:** This is NOT automatic - most people with peanut allergy don't react to lupin, but the risk is significant enough to warrant caution.

**For This App:**
- If user has peanut allergy ‚Üí ALWAYS warn about lupin cross-reactivity risk
- Suggest they've been tested for lupin allergy before consuming
- Recommend consulting allergist if uncertain
- DO NOT assume peanut allergy = automatic lupin allergy (only 5-37% risk)
- DO warn that the risk exists and is higher than other legumes

---

### Soy and Other Legume Cross-Reactivity - LOWER RISK
**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è POSSIBLE but less common than peanut
**Explanation:** Lupin is a legume, so there's potential for cross-reactivity with other legumes (soy, chickpeas, lentils, beans). However, cross-reactivity with other legumes is much less common than with peanuts.
**For This App:**
- If user has lupin allergy ‚Üí they can usually eat other legumes safely
- However, if they have multiple legume allergies ‚Üí be cautious with all legumes
- Recommend allergy testing to determine which legumes are safe

---

### Gluten-Free Products - INCREASING RISK
**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è INCREASING use of lupin in gluten-free products
**Explanation:** Lupin flour is naturally gluten-free, high in protein, and increasingly popular as a wheat substitute in gluten-free baking. This means people with coeliac disease or gluten intolerance may encounter lupin more frequently.
**Common gluten-free products with lupin:**
- Gluten-free bread (some brands)
- Gluten-free pasta (especially high-protein varieties)
- Gluten-free baked goods
- Gluten-free flour blends (some)

**For This App:**
- If user has both gluten AND peanut allergies ‚Üí extra vigilant about gluten-free products
- Always check labels on gluten-free products for lupin
- Suggest UK brands that typically don't use lupin (rice/corn based)

---

### European vs UK Products - GEOGRAPHIC VARIATION
**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è MORE COMMON in European products than UK
**Explanation:** Lupin is more commonly used in France, Italy, Germany, and Mediterranean countries than in the UK. White lupin is commonly grown in Mediterranean regions.
**France:** First lupin anaphylaxis reported in 1999; by 2002, lupin was the 4th most frequent cause of severe food-associated anaphylaxis.
**UK:** Less common in UK-manufactured products, but increasing in gluten-free and health food products.

**For This App:**
- European bakery products ‚Üí higher risk
- Continental pastries, bread, pasta ‚Üí higher risk
- UK supermarket standard products ‚Üí lower risk (but check labels)
- Imported foods from Mediterranean countries ‚Üí check labels
- Health food shops ‚Üí more likely to stock lupin products than mainstream shops

### Mislabeling Risk - HIDDEN ALLERGEN
**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è Can be overlooked or mislabeled
**Explanation:** Because lupin is less well-known than peanuts or gluten, some people (and even some food businesses) may not recognize it as a major allergen. However, UK law requires lupin to be clearly labeled.
**Risks:**
- Homemade products at markets (may not know about lupin)
- Small bakeries (especially European-style)
- Restaurant dishes (may use lupin flour without realizing it's an allergen)

**For This App:**
- Always recommend asking about lupin in restaurants
- Packaged foods in UK must declare lupin by law
- Be extra cautious with homemade/craft products
