## Background: What are Crustaceans?

Crustaceans are shellfish with segmented bodies, jointed legs, and hard exoskeletons. They are one of the 14 major allergens regulated by the UK Food Standards Agency and must be clearly labeled on all food products.

**Common crustaceans include:**
- Prawns, Shrimp
- Crab
- Lobster
- Crayfish, Crawfish
- Langoustine (Dublin Bay Prawns, Scampi)
- Krill

**Allergen protein:** Tropomyosin is the main allergenic protein in crustaceans, causing reactions in over 60% of shellfish-allergic patients.

**Important:** Crustacean allergies are typically lifelong and rarely outgrown. Reactions can be severe and may occur from eating, touching, or inhaling cooking vapors.

**Note:** In the UK, "shellfish" refers to both:
- **Crustaceans** (prawns, crab, lobster) - covered in this document
- **Molluscs** (mussels, oysters, clams) - separate allergen (see separate taxonomy)

### Fish (Different Allergen)

**Important:** Fish is a SEPARATE allergen from crustaceans. Many people allergic to crustaceans can safely eat fish (though some may be allergic to both).

**All Fish Are Crustacean-Free:**
- White fish (cod, haddock, plaice, sole)
- Oily fish (salmon, mackerel, tuna, sardines)
- Freshwater fish (trout, carp, pike)
- All fish varieties

**Caution:**
- Some fish dishes may be cooked with shellfish (paella, bouillabaisse)
- Shared fryers may cause cross-contamination
- Fish sauce may contain shrimp

### Molluscs (Different Allergen - But Related)

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è Molluscs are a SEPARATE allergen from crustaceans in UK law

**Molluscs include:**
- Mussels, Oysters, Clams
- Scallops, Whelks, Winkles
- Squid (calamari), Octopus, Cuttlefish
- Snails (escargot)

**Important cross-reactivity note:**
- Up to 75% of people with crustacean allergy may also react to molluscs (due to tropomyosin)
- Some crustacean-allergic people CAN safely eat molluscs
- Always check with allergist before trying molluscs if you have crustacean allergy
- For recipe app: TREAT CAUTIOUSLY - flag as potential cross-reactivity risk

## ‚ö†Ô∏è TRICKY CASES - Important Notes

### Crustaceans vs Molluscs - Related But SEPARATE Allergens

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è SEPARATE allergens in UK law, BUT can cross-react

**The Key Difference:**
- **Crustaceans:** Hard segmented shell, jointed legs (prawns, crab, lobster)
- **Molluscs:** Soft body, often in one shell or no shell (mussels, oysters, squid)

**UK Law:** Both must be declared separately on food labels
- "Contains: Crustaceans"
- "Contains: Molluscs"

**Cross-Reactivity:**
- Both contain tropomyosin (same allergenic protein)
- Up to 75% of crustacean-allergic people also react to molluscs
- 25% can safely eat molluscs despite crustacean allergy
- Some people are allergic to molluscs but not crustaceans

**For This App:**
If user has crustacean allergy:
- FLAG mollusc-containing recipes as "potential cross-reactivity risk"
- Suggest checking with allergist before trying molluscs
- Don't automatically exclude molluscs, but WARN user

**Examples:**
- Prawns (crustacean) vs Mussels (mollusc)
- Crab (crustacean) vs Oysters (mollusc)
- Lobster (crustacean) vs Squid/Calamari (mollusc)

### Prawn Crackers - ALWAYS Contain Shrimp

**Status:** ‚ùå UNSAFE - contains crustaceans

**Explanation:** Despite being called "crackers," prawn crackers (krupuk udang) ALWAYS contain real shrimp or prawns. They are made from tapioca starch mixed with ground shrimp.

**Common Misconception:** "It's just the flavor" - NO, they contain real shrimp!

**UK Availability:**
- Chinese restaurants and takeaways
- Supermarkets (Asian food aisles)
- Some fish and chip shops

**Alternative:** Plain prawn crackers do not exist - look for plain rice crackers or vegetable crackers instead.

---

### Fish Sauce - OFTEN Contains Shrimp

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è OFTEN UNSAFE - frequently contains shrimp/prawns

**Explanation:** Traditional fish sauce (nam pla, nuoc mam) is made by fermenting fish. However, many brands also include shrimp or prawns in the fermentation process.

**UK Products:**
- Some Thai and Vietnamese fish sauce brands contain shrimp
- Must be declared on label in UK ("Contains: Fish, Crustaceans")
- Vegetarian/vegan fish sauce is safe (made from soy or mushrooms)

**Always check labels:** Even if the main ingredient is fish, shrimp may be added.

**Safe Alternatives:**
- Soy sauce (check it's just soy and wheat)
- Coconut aminos
- Vegan fish sauce (mushroom-based)

---

### Prawn/Seafood Flavored Crisps - USUALLY Safe

**Status:** ‚úÖ USUALLY SAFE (but check labels)

**Explanation:** Most "prawn cocktail" flavored crisps in the UK do NOT contain real prawns - they use artificial flavorings.

**UK Examples:**
- Walkers Prawn Cocktail - Usually safe (artificial flavor)
- Skips Prawn Cocktail - Usually safe (artificial flavor)

**BUT:**
- Always check the allergen label
- Some premium or Asian-style crisps may contain real shrimp powder
- If label says "Contains: Crustaceans" ‚Üí UNSAFE

**How to check:** Look at allergen box on pack:
- If no crustacean warning ‚Üí Usually safe
- If says "Contains: Crustaceans" ‚Üí UNSAFE

### Glucosamine Supplements - Often Shellfish-Derived

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è OFTEN UNSAFE - usually derived from crustacean shells

**Explanation:** Glucosamine supplements (used for joint health) are typically made from the shells of shrimp, crab, or lobster.

**UK Guidance:**
- Allergy UK advises avoiding glucosamine if shellfish-allergic
- UK licensed brands (Alateris, Dolenio, Glusartel, YOINTY) contraindicate use in shellfish allergy
- May contain trace proteins despite being from shells (not flesh)

**Scientific Note:**
- Shellfish allergy is typically to flesh proteins, not shell
- However, cross-contamination can occur during processing
- Risk cannot be completely ruled out

**Safe Alternatives:**
- Shellfish-free glucosamine (made from fermented corn or fungi)
- Look for "suitable for shellfish allergy" on label
- Plant-based joint supplements

**Recommendation:** Avoid unless specifically labeled "shellfish-free"

---

### Crab Sticks (Surimi) - COMPLICATED

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è CHECK CAREFULLY - may or may not contain crab

**Explanation:** "Crab sticks" or "seafood sticks" are made from surimi (processed fish paste), usually pollock or cod. They are typically flavored to taste like crab.

**UK Products:**
- Most UK crab sticks do NOT contain real crab (just fish)
- However, some premium brands add real crab extract for flavor
- Must be declared on label if contains crustaceans

**How to check:**
- Read allergen box: "Contains: Fish" only ‚Üí Safe (no crab)
- Read allergen box: "Contains: Fish, Crustaceans" ‚Üí UNSAFE (contains crab)

**Note:** Even if safe from crustaceans, crab sticks contain fish (separate allergen).

### Asian Sauces and Pastes - High Risk for Hidden Shellfish

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è OFTEN UNSAFE - frequently contain shrimp paste

**Explanation:** Many Asian sauces, curry pastes, and condiments contain shrimp paste (belacan, terasi, kapi) as a base ingredient.

**High-Risk Products:**
- Thai curry pastes (red, green, yellow) - OFTEN contain shrimp paste
- Malaysian sambal (many varieties contain belacan)
- Indonesian bumbu (seasoning pastes)
- Some kimchi (contains salted shrimp)
- Some oyster sauce brands (may contain shrimp)

**Always Check Labels:**
- Look for "shrimp paste," "belacan," "terasi," "kapi"
- Check allergen box for "Crustaceans"
- When in doubt, ASK in restaurants

**Safe Alternatives:**
- Vegan/vegetarian Asian curry pastes (specify no shrimp paste)
- Make your own curry paste from scratch
- Japanese miso paste (soy-based, no shellfish)

### Shared Equipment and Cross-Contamination

**High-Risk Situations:**

**Fish Markets and Counters:**
- Shared cutting boards, knives, scales
- Crustacean juices can contaminate fish
- Ask for thoroughly cleaned equipment

**Shared Fryers:**
- Chips/fries cooked in same oil as scampi/prawns
- Battered fish may be cooked with battered prawns
- Fish and chip shops - ASK about separate fryers

**Asian Restaurants:**
- Shrimp paste used in many dishes (even vegetable dishes)
- Shared woks and cooking utensils
- Cross-contamination risk is HIGH

**Seafood Restaurants:**
- Shared grills, pans, cooking surfaces
- Airborne proteins from steaming shellfish
- High cross-contamination risk

**For This App:**
If user has severe crustacean allergy:
- Warn about cross-contamination risks
- Suggest dedicated cooking equipment
- Advise caution in seafood restaurants

### Chitin and Chitosan - Derived from Crustacean Shells

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è UNSAFE - derived from shellfish shells

**Explanation:** Chitin and chitosan are derived from the shells of crustaceans (mainly shrimp, crab). They are used in dietary supplements, cosmetics, and some food products.

**Uses:**
- Dietary fiber supplements
- Weight loss supplements
- Wound dressings (medical)
- Some cosmetics
- Food preservatives (rarely)

**UK Labeling:**
- Must be declared if used in food products
- Check supplement labels carefully

**Allergy Risk:**
- Similar to glucosamine - risk is debated
- May contain trace proteins from processing
- Allergists typically recommend avoidance

**Recommendation:** Avoid unless confirmed safe by allergist

### "Seafood" - Ambiguous Term

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è CLARIFY - could mean fish, crustaceans, molluscs, or all

**Explanation:** "Seafood" is not specific enough. It can refer to:
- Fish only
- Crustaceans only
- Molluscs only
- All of the above

**In Recipes:**
- "Seafood stock" - Could contain any combination
- "Seafood mix" - Usually contains crustaceans and molluscs
- "Seafood sauce" - Check ingredients

**UK Labeling:**
- Allergen labels must specify: "Fish," "Crustaceans," or "Molluscs"
- Generic "seafood" is not sufficient for allergen declaration

**For This App:**
When you see "seafood" without specification:
- FLAG as "may contain crustaceans - please specify exact ingredients"
- Ask user to clarify what type of seafood
- Better to be cautious

### Cooking Vapors and Airborne Exposure

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è CAUTION - can trigger reactions in some people

**Explanation:** Some highly sensitive individuals may react to airborne proteins released when shellfish is cooked (steaming, boiling, frying).

**Where This Occurs:**
- Home cooking (boiling prawns, steaming crab)
- Fish markets (fresh shellfish)
- Seafood restaurants (cooking shellfish nearby)
- Asian restaurants (wok cooking with shrimp paste)

**Symptoms:**
- Respiratory symptoms (wheezing, coughing)
- Skin reactions (hives, itching)
- Eye irritation
- In severe cases, anaphylaxis

**Recommendation for Severe Allergy:**
- Avoid being in the kitchen when shellfish is cooking
- Avoid fish markets and seafood counters
- Be cautious in seafood restaurants (inform staff)
- Ensure good ventilation if others cook shellfish at home
