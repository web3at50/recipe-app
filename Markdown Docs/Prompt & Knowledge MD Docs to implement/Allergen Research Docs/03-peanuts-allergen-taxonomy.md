## Background: What is Peanut Allergy?

Peanut allergy is one of the most common food allergies and a leading cause of anaphylaxis in the UK. Important facts:

- **Peanuts are LEGUMES** (in the same plant family as peas, beans, and lentils), **NOT tree nuts**
- Affects approximately **1 in 50 children** and **1 in 200 adults** in the UK
- Can cause severe allergic reactions, including life-threatening **anaphylaxis**
- Reactions can be triggered by **tiny amounts** (a fragment of peanut is enough)
- Peanut allergy is one of the **14 major allergens** that must be declared on UK food labels
- **30-40% of children** with peanut allergy may also have tree nut allergies, but they are **separate allergens**

**Alternative Names for Peanuts:**
- Groundnut
- Monkey nuts
- Arachis (scientific name: Arachis hypogaea)
- Beer nuts (when roasted and salted)
- Goober nuts

For this app, treat all peanut exposure as requiring strict avoidance.

## ‚ö†Ô∏è TRICKY CASES - Important Notes

### Peanuts vs Tree Nuts - DIFFERENT ALLERGENS

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è DIFFERENT allergens - separate plant families

**Explanation:**
- **Peanuts** = Legumes (family: Fabaceae) - related to peas, beans, lentils
- **Tree nuts** = Various botanical families - almonds, walnuts, cashews, hazelnuts, etc.
- These are **completely different plant families**
- A peanut allergy does NOT automatically mean tree nut allergy

**However:**
- **30-40% of children** with peanut allergy also have tree nut allergies
- This is called **co-allergy** or **cross-reactivity**
- Always verify with allergy testing before assuming tree nuts are safe
- If uncertain, avoid both peanuts AND tree nuts

**UK Labeling:**
- Peanuts and tree nuts are **separate allergens** in the UK's 14 major allergens
- Labels must specify "peanuts" AND "tree nuts" separately
- Generic "may contain nuts" is discouraged - labels should specify "may contain peanuts" or "may contain tree nuts"

---

### Refined Peanut Oil - CONTROVERSIAL

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è Likely safe for most, but MUST be declared on UK labels

**The Science:**
- A landmark 1997 Southampton study tested 60 peanut-allergic individuals
- **NONE reacted to refined peanut oil**
- 10% reacted to crude/unrefined peanut oil
- Refined oils contain **no detectable protein** (allergens are proteins)
- Crude "gourmet" oils retain flavor and **may contain protein**

**The UK/EU Regulation:**
- Despite evidence, **peanut oil MUST be declared** on labels (refined or unrefined)
- This is different from the US FDA, which exempts highly refined oils
- EFSA assessment determined peanut oil must always be declared

**Recommendation:**
- **Refined peanut oil** is likely safe for the overwhelming majority of peanut-allergic people
- **Unrefined/crude/cold-pressed peanut oil** is UNSAFE
- It's up to individuals/parents to weigh the evidence and decide
- If uncertain, avoid all peanut oil

**How to Identify:**
- "Refined peanut oil" or "highly refined peanut oil" = likely safe
- "Cold-pressed peanut oil," "gourmet peanut oil," "crude peanut oil," or just "arachis oil" = UNSAFE

### Peanuts and Other Legumes - USUALLY SAFE

**Status:** ‚úÖ Most people with peanut allergy can eat other legumes

**Explanation:**
- Peanuts are legumes (like peas, beans, lentils, soybeans)
- However, **most people with peanut allergy can safely eat other legumes**
- The proteins in peanuts are different from those in other legumes
- Cross-reactivity is rare but possible

**Safe for Most:**
- Chickpeas, lentils, peas, beans, soybeans (edamame, tofu)
- Hummus (chickpea-based)
- Soy milk, soy sauce (if no soy allergy)

**Exception - Lupin:**
- Lupin is a legume that shows **higher cross-reactivity** with peanuts
- Lupin is one of the UK's 14 major allergens
- Some people with peanut allergy may react to lupin
- Check labels for lupin flour (used in some European baked goods)

**Recommendation:**
- Most peanut-allergic individuals can eat other legumes safely
- Introduce cautiously if uncertain
- Consult an allergist if you have concerns

### Cross-Contamination and "May Contain" Labels

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è Take "may contain peanuts" warnings seriously

**Explanation:**
- "May contain peanuts" means the product is made in a facility or on equipment that also processes peanuts
- Cross-contamination can occur even with thorough cleaning
- Peanut allergy can be triggered by **tiny amounts** (a fragment)

**High-Risk Cross-Contamination:**
- Bakeries (shared mixers, trays, work surfaces)
- Ice cream shops (shared scoops)
- Bulk bins in stores (scoops, airborne particles)
- Nut-free products made in facilities that also process peanuts
- Takeaway restaurants (shared woks, fryers, utensils)

**Recommendation:**
- Avoid products with "may contain peanuts" warnings if highly sensitive
- Use separate toasters, cutting boards, and utensils at home
- Ask restaurants about cross-contamination procedures
- Be extra cautious with takeaway food (Chinese, Thai, Indian)

### Arachis in Cosmetics

**Status:** ‚ö†Ô∏è May be present in skincare products

**Explanation:**
- Arachis oil (peanut oil) is sometimes used in cosmetics and skincare
- EU/UK regulation limits peanut protein in cosmetics to **0.5 ppm** (2017 amendment)
- Ingredients may be listed as "Arachis hypogaea oil" or "peanut oil"

**Products That May Contain:**
- Lotions, creams, moisturizers
- Shampoos and conditioners
- Makeup products
- Lip balms

**Recommendation:**
- Check cosmetic ingredient lists for "arachis" or "peanut"
- Peanut proteins are limited but not eliminated
- If highly sensitive, choose products explicitly peanut-free

**Status:** ‚ùå UNSAFE - contains peanuts

**Explanation:**
- "Beer nuts" are simply salted roasted peanuts
- The name refers to them being served with beer, NOT made from beer
