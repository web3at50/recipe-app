# Comprehensive Research Report: Natasha's Law and UK Food Allergen Regulations for Recipe Applications

**Research Date:** October 14, 2025
**Purpose:** To inform the development of allergen information collection and management features for a recipe and meal planning application

---

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive research report examines Natasha's Law, UK food allergen regulations, and their application to digital food services including recipe and meal planning applications. The research reveals critical information about the 14 major food allergens, legal requirements for food businesses, and best practices for allergen management in digital platforms.

**Key Findings:**

- **Natasha's Law** came into force on October 1, 2021, requiring full ingredient labelling with emphasized allergens for Prepacked for Direct Sale (PPDS) foods
- The law was named after **Natasha Ednan-Laperouse**, who died aged 15 in 2016 from an allergic reaction to undeclared sesame in a Pret a Manger baguette
- **14 major food allergens** are regulated under UK law, affecting an estimated 2.4 million UK adults with food allergies
- While recipe apps are not directly regulated under Natasha's Law, they have a **duty of care** to provide accurate allergen information and clear disclaimers
- **10 people die annually** in the UK from food-induced anaphylaxis due to undeclared allergens, with young adults being the highest risk group
- Food allergy prevalence has **more than doubled** in the UK over the past decade
- Hospital admissions for anaphylaxis have increased by **108% from 2002-2023**, while death rates have declined due to better management

## 1. Natasha's Law: Background and Legal Requirements

### 1.1 The Tragic Incident

**Natasha Ednan-Laperouse** was a 15-year-old girl who died on July 17, 2016, after eating an artichoke, olive and tapenade baguette purchased from Pret A Manger at Heathrow Airport. The baguette contained sesame seeds baked into the dough, but this ingredient was not listed on the packaging.

**Timeline of Events:**

- **Approximately 30 minutes after takeoff**, Natasha began feeling ill and complained of an itchy throat
- She broke out in hives on her neck and midriff, and her breathing became labored
- Her final words to her father were: **"Daddy please help me I can't breathe"**
- Her father administered two EpiPen injections; a junior doctor on board administered additional epinephrine
- Natasha suffered cardiac arrest and lost consciousness
- She was met by French paramedics upon landing and taken to hospital, where she was pronounced dead

**The Inquest (September 2018):**

- The coroner called Pret a Manger's allergy labelling **"inadequate"**
- It emerged that Pret had received a **"specific warning"** about the dangers of not signposting allergens the previous year
- The tragedy highlighted a critical gap in UK food labelling regulations

### 1.2 Legal Timeline and Implementation

**Key Dates:**

- **July 17, 2016:** Natasha Ednan-Laperouse dies from allergic reaction
- **September 2018:** Inquest concludes, highlighting inadequate allergen labelling
- **2019:** Pret a Manger becomes the first food-to-go business to introduce full ingredient labels on freshly made in-store products
- **July 2019:** New legislation passed
- **October 1, 2021:** Natasha's Law comes into full legal effect across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland

### 1.3 Official Name and Legal Framework

**Official Designation:**
- The Food Information (Amendment) (England) Regulations 2019
- Commonly known as **"Natasha's Law"**

**Legal Framework:**
- Amends the **Food Information Regulations 2014** (FIR)
- Implements requirements from **retained EU Regulation 1169/2011** on the provision of food information to consumers
- Enforced by local authorities across the UK

### 1.4 Which Businesses It Applies To

Natasha's Law applies to **all food businesses** selling **Prepacked for Direct Sale (PPDS)** foods, regardless of size, including:

- Cafes and coffee shops
- Bakeries
- Delicatessens
- Sandwich bars
- Supermarkets with in-store preparation
- Food stalls and markets
- School and workplace canteens
- Restaurants offering pre-packed items
- Takeaway establishments

**Definition of PPDS:**
Food that is packaged on the same premises from which it is sold, before being ordered or selected by the customer.

### 1.5 Key Requirements for Food Businesses

**Mandatory Label Information:**

1. **Name of the food** - clearly identifying what the product is
2. **Full ingredients list** - every ingredient must be listed
3. **Allergen emphasis** - all 14 major allergens must be emphasized (e.g., in bold, italics, or highlighted) each time they appear in the ingredients list

**Example Format:**
```
Artichoke, Olive & Tapenade Baguette

Ingredients: Wheat flour, water, artichokes (10%), olives (8%),
tapenade (contains SESAME, anchovies (FISH), capers), olive oil,
salt, yeast.

Allergens: Contains WHEAT, SESAME, FISH
```

**Additional Compliance Requirements:**

- Information must be easily visible and clearly legible
- Labels must not be removable or easily damaged
- Staff must be trained on allergen awareness
- Records of ingredients, recipes, and specifications must be maintained
- Risk assessments for cross-contamination must be conducted

### 1.6 The Owen Carey Case and "Owen's Law" Campaign

**Background:**
Owen Carey died on April 22, 2017, at age 18, after eating a Byron burger while celebrating his birthday. He had informed staff of his dairy allergy, but the menu did not indicate that the chicken burger was marinated in buttermilk.

**Coroner's Ruling:**
"The deceased made serving staff aware of his allergies. The menu was reassuring in that it made no reference to any marinade."

**Owen's Law Campaign:**
The Carey family campaigned for restaurants to display **clear allergen information on each individual dish on their menus**, arguing that verbal communication in busy, noisy restaurants with high staff turnover leaves too much room for error.

**Current Status:**
While Owen's Law has not been enacted as legislation, the Food Standards Agency released updated guidance in March 2025 recommending that businesses provide written allergen information for non-prepacked foods through printed menus, allergen charts, posters, or digital resources.

## 2. The 14 Major Food Allergens

The UK Food Standards Agency (FSA) recognizes 14 allergens as the most common ingredients that cause food allergies and intolerances. These are listed in Annex II of the Food Information to Consumers (FIC) regulation.

### Complete List with Detailed Descriptions

#### 1. CELERY

**What it includes:**
- Celery stalks
- Celery leaves
- Celery seeds
- Celeriac (celery root)

**Common foods containing celery:**
- Celery salt and seasonings
- Salads and coleslaw
- Meat products (sausages, burgers)
- Soups and broths
- Stock cubes and bouillon
- Vegetable juices

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Celery allergies can cause reactions ranging from mild oral allergy syndrome (itchiness in the throat and mouth) to severe anaphylaxis in sensitive individuals. Celery is often used as a flavoring agent and can be hidden in processed foods.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Celery is often processed alongside other vegetables, and celery seeds can contaminate spice mixes.

---

#### 2. CEREALS CONTAINING GLUTEN

**What it includes:**
- Wheat (all varieties including spelt, kamut)
- Rye
- Barley
- Oats
- Their hybridized strains

**Common foods containing gluten:**
- Bread, cakes, and pastries
- Pasta and noodles
- Baking powder and batters
- Breadcrumbs and coatings
- Couscous and bulgur wheat
- Flour (including as a thickener)
- Many processed and fried foods
- Beer and malt vinegar
- Sauces and gravies
- Breakfast cereals

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Gluten-containing cereals can cause:
- **Celiac disease** - an autoimmune condition affecting approximately 1% of the UK population
- **Wheat allergy** - an IgE-mediated allergic reaction
- **Non-celiac gluten sensitivity** - adverse reactions without celiac disease

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Gluten is extremely difficult to eliminate from shared facilities. Even trace amounts can trigger reactions in celiac disease sufferers. Oats are often cross-contaminated with wheat during processing.

---

#### 3. CRUSTACEANS

**What it includes:**
- Prawns and shrimp
- Crabs
- Lobsters
- Crayfish
- Scampi
- Langoustines

**Common foods containing crustaceans:**
- Seafood dishes and paella
- Asian cuisine (spring rolls, crackers)
- Fish stock and bouillon
- Seafood sauces and pastes
- Some Worcestershire sauces
- Surimi (imitation crab)

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Crustacean allergy is one of the most common food allergies, particularly in adults. Reactions tend to be severe and potentially life-threatening. Notably, allergic reactions can be triggered not just by eating shellfish but also by **airborne proteins released during cooking**, meaning sensitive individuals can react to shellfish cooking vapors.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Shellfish proteins are highly allergenic and persistent. Shared fryers, grills, and cooking surfaces pose significant risks. The proteins can become airborne during cooking.

---

#### 4. EGGS

**What it includes:**
- Chicken eggs
- Duck eggs
- Quail eggs
- Goose eggs
- All egg products (whites, yolks, whole eggs)

**Common foods containing eggs:**
- Cakes, biscuits, and pastries
- Mayonnaise and salad dressings
- Mousses and meringues
- Pasta (fresh and some dried)
- Quiche and egg-based dishes
- Sauces (hollandaise, b√©arnaise)
- Foods brushed or glazed with egg
- Some meat products
- Ice cream and custards
- Marshmallows

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Eggs are **one of the most common foods to trigger allergic symptoms in babies and young children**. According to the NHS, many cases of egg allergy are relatively mild, with symptoms including aggravated asthma and abdominal pain. Most children outgrow egg allergy by school age, but some individuals remain allergic throughout life.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Egg proteins can be found in unexpected places. Eggs are used as binding agents, emulsifiers, and glazes. Some vaccines are cultured in eggs (flu vaccine).

---

#### 5. FISH

**What it includes:**
- All types of fish (cod, haddock, salmon, tuna, mackerel, etc.)
- Fish products and derivatives

**Common foods containing fish:**
- Fish sauces (Asian cuisine)
- Worcestershire sauce
- Caesar salad dressing (anchovies)
- Pizza toppings (anchovies)
- Relishes and condiments
- Stock cubes and bouillon
- Surimi (fish-based)
- Some vitamins and supplements (omega-3)

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Fish allergy is typically lifelong and reactions can be severe. Unlike shellfish allergy, fish allergy usually means all fish must be avoided (though some individuals may tolerate certain species). Fish proteins can become airborne during cooking.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Fish proteins are highly stable and can contaminate shared cooking equipment, utensils, and surfaces. The smell of fish often indicates airborne proteins that can trigger reactions.

---

#### 6. LUPIN

**What it includes:**
- Lupin beans and seeds
- Lupin flour
- Lupin protein

**Common foods containing lupin:**
- Some types of bread and baked goods
- Pastries and pancakes
- Pasta (some varieties)
- Sauces and gravies
- Meat substitutes and vegetarian products
- Gluten-free products
- Some snack foods

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Lupin is a legume related to peanuts. People with peanut allergies have a higher risk of reacting to lupin. Lupin flour is increasingly used in gluten-free and high-protein products, making it an emerging allergen concern.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Lupin is often processed in facilities that handle other legumes and nuts. It's becoming more common in specialty foods.

---

#### 7. MILK

**What it includes:**
- Cow's milk
- Goat's milk
- Sheep's milk
- All dairy products (butter, cheese, cream, yogurt)
- Milk powders and proteins (whey, casein, lactose)

**Common foods containing milk:**
- Butter, cheese, and cream
- Milk powders and condensed milk
- Yogurt and kefir
- Ice cream and frozen desserts
- Chocolate and confectionery
- Baked goods (cakes, biscuits)
- Sauces (b√©chamel, carbonara)
- Foods brushed or glazed with milk
- Powdered soups and sauces
- Some processed meats
- Some margarine and spreads

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Milk allergy is particularly common in infants and young children. Importantly, research shows that **cow's milk, not peanuts or tree nuts, is responsible for the most fatal allergic reactions** in the UK. Milk allergy is different from lactose intolerance (which is not an allergy but an enzyme deficiency).

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Dairy proteins are pervasive in food processing. "May contain milk" warnings are common. Some non-dairy products may be processed on shared equipment.

---

#### 8. MOLLUSCS

**What it includes:**
- Mussels
- Oysters
- Clams
- Scallops
- Squid and octopus
- Land snails (escargot)
- Whelks and cockles
- Cuttlefish

**Common foods containing molluscs:**
- Oyster sauce (Asian cuisine)
- Fish stews and bouillabaisse
- Paella and seafood rice dishes
- Seafood pasta
- Calamari and fried squid

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Mollusc allergy is common in adults and can cause severe reactions. Unlike crustaceans, mollusc allergy may not cause reactions to all types (some people can tolerate certain molluscs but not others).

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Molluscs are often prepared alongside other seafood. Shared fryers and cooking surfaces pose risks.

---

#### 9. MUSTARD

**What it includes:**
- Mustard seeds
- Mustard powder
- Liquid mustard (Dijon, wholegrain, English)
- Mustard leaves

**Common foods containing mustard:**
- Mustard condiments
- Salad dressings and vinaigrettes
- Marinades and rubs
- Sauces (honey mustard, barbecue)
- Curries and Indian dishes
- Pickles and relishes
- Meat products (sausages, burgers)
- Soups
- Breads (some varieties)

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Mustard allergy can cause reactions ranging from mild to severe anaphylaxis. It's particularly common in countries where mustard is widely consumed. Mustard is often used as a flavor enhancer and can be hidden in processed foods.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Mustard seeds can contaminate spice mixes and seasonings. It's a common ingredient in commercial food production.

---

#### 10. PEANUTS

**What it includes:**
- Peanuts (groundnuts)
- Peanut butter and peanut oil
- Peanut flour and protein

**Common foods containing peanuts:**
- Peanut butter and spreads
- Satay sauce and Asian dishes
- Confectionery and chocolate bars
- Cookies and baked goods
- Breakfast cereals and granola
- Ice cream and desserts
- Vegetarian products (meat substitutes)
- Some sauces and marinades
- Trail mix and snack foods
- Some African and Asian cuisines

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Peanut allergy is one of the most common and severe food allergies. **Almost half of all deaths from anaphylaxis are related to peanuts or tree nuts**. Signs and symptoms can occur within minutes of contact but can take up to an hour to be apparent. An allergy to peanuts increases the likelihood of developing allergies to tree nuts, sesame seeds, and lupin.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Peanuts are highly allergenic even in trace amounts. Shared manufacturing equipment poses significant risks. "May contain peanuts" warnings are extremely common on packaged foods.

---

#### 11. SESAME

**What it includes:**
- Sesame seeds
- Sesame oil
- Tahini (sesame paste)
- Halva

**Common foods containing sesame:**
- Bread and breadsticks (often as garnish)
- Hummus
- Tahini and halva
- Sesame oil (cooking and flavoring)
- Asian cuisine (sushi, stir-fries)
- Middle Eastern foods
- Burger buns and rolls
- Salad dressings
- Vegetarian and vegan products
- Some crackers and snack foods

**Why it's a major allergen:**
**Sesame allergy is becoming increasingly common and is now in the top 10 of UK allergens**, due to increased consumption. This was the allergen that tragically killed Natasha Ednan-Laperouse. Sesame can cause severe anaphylactic reactions.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Sesame seeds are small and easily spread in food preparation areas. They're commonly used as toppings and can contaminate baked goods. Sesame oil can be used in cooking without clear labeling.

---

#### 12. SOYBEANS (SOYA)

**What it includes:**
- Soybeans and edamame
- Soy milk and soy protein
- Soy flour and soy lecithin
- Tofu and tempeh
- Miso and soy sauce

**Common foods containing soya:**
- Bean curd and tofu
- Edamame
- Miso paste and soup
- Soy sauce and tamari
- Soya flour and protein
- Vegetarian and vegan products
- Some desserts and ice cream
- Many meat products
- Sauces and marinades
- Some baked goods
- Margarine and vegetable oils
- Processed foods (soy lecithin as emulsifier)

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Soya allergy is common in infants but often outgrown by age 10. However, it can persist into adulthood. Soya is ubiquitous in processed foods, making it challenging to avoid. Some individuals with peanut allergy may also react to soya.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Soya derivatives (especially soy lecithin) are found in countless processed foods. Shared processing facilities for legumes are common.

---

#### 13. SULPHUR DIOXIDE AND SULPHITES (above 10mg/kg or 10mg/L)

**What it includes:**
- Sulphur dioxide (SO2)
- Sulphites (E220-E228)
- Sulphating agents used as preservatives

**Common foods containing sulphites:**
- Dried fruits (raisins, apricots, prunes)
- Wine, beer, and cider
- Some fruit juices and soft drinks
- Vinegar and pickled foods
- Processed meat products
- Some vegetables (particularly preserved)
- Grape juice and grape products
- Potato products
- Some seafood (preserved shrimp)

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Sulphur dioxide is **a particular problem for many asthma sufferers**, as it can trigger severe respiratory reactions. While technically a food additive rather than a protein allergen, it can cause serious adverse reactions including breathing difficulties, hives, and anaphylaxis in sensitive individuals.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Sulphites are preservatives and less likely to cross-contaminate, but they can be present in unexpected foods like pre-cut potatoes or salad bars.

**What it includes:**
- Almonds
- Hazelnuts (filberts)
- Walnuts
- Brazil nuts
- Cashews
- Pecans
- Pistachios
- Macadamia nuts
- Queensland nuts
- Also includes nut oils and butters

**Common foods containing tree nuts:**
- Nut butters and spreads
- Marzipan (almonds)
- Pesto (pine nuts)
- Nut oils
- Praline and nougat
- Chocolate and confectionery
- Baked goods (cakes, cookies)
- Breakfast cereals and granola
- Ice cream and desserts
- Some vegetarian products
- Some liqueurs (Amaretto, Frangelico)
- Salads and vegetable dishes
- Some curries and Asian dishes

**Why it's a major allergen:**
Tree nut allergy is typically lifelong and often severe. **Almost half of all deaths from anaphylaxis are related to peanuts or tree nuts**. The **most common culprits for allergic reactions include hazelnuts, walnuts, and almonds**, along with fresh fruits. People allergic to one tree nut are often allergic to others, so medical advice typically recommends avoiding all tree nuts.

**Cross-contamination considerations:**
Tree nuts are highly allergenic in trace amounts. Shared processing equipment for nuts is common. Nut oils can contain allergenic proteins. "May contain traces of nuts" warnings are prevalent on packaged foods.

## 3. Application to Digital Food Services and Recipe Apps

### 3.1 Legal Context for Recipe Apps

While **Natasha's Law specifically applies to PPDS foods** (physically packaged and sold from the same premises), digital food services including recipe and meal planning apps operate in a related regulatory environment:

**Applicable Regulations:**
- **Food Information Regulations 2014** - General principles of food information accuracy
- **Consumer Rights Act 2015** - Services must be performed with reasonable care and skill
- **Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR** - For collection and storage of personal health information (allergies)
- **Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008** - Prohibition on misleading information

**Key Principle:**
Recipe apps have a **duty of care** to provide accurate information and not mislead users about allergen content, even though they are not selling physical food products.

### 3.2 Responsibility and Liability Considerations

**Legal Responsibilities:**

1. **Accuracy of Information**
   - Recipe apps must ensure allergen information is accurate and up-to-date
   - Sources of recipes should be verified and reliable
   - Regular audits of recipe database for allergen accuracy

2. **Transparency and Disclaimers**
   - Clear disclaimers about the limitations of allergen information
   - Statements encouraging users to verify ingredients independently
   - Transparency about data sources and update frequency

3. **User Safety**
   - Prominent warnings about cross-contamination risks in home cooking
   - Education about the severity of food allergies
   - Emergency guidance (when to use EpiPen, when to call emergency services)

4. **Data Protection**
   - Allergen information is health data under UK GDPR
   - Requires explicit consent and clear purpose statements
   - Must be stored securely with appropriate data protection measures

**Liability Considerations:**

While disclaimers can help reduce liability, they **do not eliminate** a food service's legal responsibility. Product liability issues can arise from:

- **Negligence** - Failure to act with reasonable care in labeling allergens
- **Misrepresentation** - Providing inaccurate or misleading allergen information
- **Data Breaches** - Improper handling of sensitive health information

**Best Practice Approach:**
Recipe apps should combine:
- Comprehensive, accurate allergen data
- Clear, prominent disclaimers
- User education about verification responsibilities
- Robust data protection measures
- Regular updates and quality assurance processes

### 3.3 FSA Recommendations for Digital Platforms (2025 Update)

In **March 2025**, the Food Standards Agency released updated recommendations that explicitly address digital platforms:

**Key Recommendations:**

1. **Written Allergen Information**
   - Apps and online platforms should provide written allergen information (not just verbal)
   - Information should be accessible before purchase or consumption decisions

2. **Digital-First Approach**
   - Apps or online platforms that allow customers to quickly access allergen information are recommended
   - Many consumers prefer to check allergen details online before making dining decisions

3. **Clarity and Consistency**
   - Allergen information should be clear, comprehensive, and accurate
   - Information must be easily available in writing to ensure accuracy and consistency

4. **Distance Selling (if applicable)**
   - If food is sold online, allergen information must be provided:
     - Before the purchase is completed
     - When the food is delivered

### 3.5 User Safety and Transparency Requirements

#### Essential Safety Features

1. **Prominent Disclaimers**
   - Displayed during onboarding and on recipe pages
   - Clear statement of app limitations
   - Encouragement to verify all ingredients

2. **Allergen Highlighting**
   - Clear visual indicators on recipes containing user's allergens
   - Color-coded warnings (e.g., red for allergens, yellow for cross-contamination risk)
   - Specific list of which allergens are present

3. **Cross-Contamination Warnings**
   - Information about shared equipment risks
   - Kitchen safety tips for preventing cross-contact
   - Warnings about airborne allergens (shellfish, fish during cooking)

## 6. The Importance of Allergen Awareness for Home Cooking

### 6.1 Why Home Cooking Matters

While Natasha's Law specifically addresses pre-packaged foods sold in commercial settings, **home cooking presents unique challenges and opportunities for allergen management**:

**Challenges:**

1. **Ingredient Complexity**
   - Packaged ingredients may contain hidden allergens
   - Manufacturers can change formulations without notice
   - Cross-contamination from shared manufacturing

2. **Cross-Contact Risks**
   - Shared cooking equipment
   - Airborne proteins during cooking (fish, shellfish)
   - Improper cleaning between dishes
   - Storage contamination

3. **Recipe Variations**
   - Recipes may not specify brand-name ingredients
   - Substitutions may introduce allergens
   - "Hidden" allergens in common ingredients

**Opportunities:**

1. **Complete Control**
   - Home cooks can verify every ingredient
   - Control over cleaning and preparation
   - Ability to create safe spaces for allergen-free cooking

2. **Customization**
   - Easier to substitute allergens
   - Adapt recipes to individual needs
   - Create family-safe versions

3. **Education**
   - Learn about allergen sources
   - Develop safe cooking practices
   - Build confidence in managing allergies

### 6.2 Cross-Contamination in Home Kitchens

**Critical Understanding:**
According to allergy organizations, **even tiny amounts of food protein can cause reactions in people with food allergies, and importantly, cooking does not reduce or eliminate the chances of a person with a food allergy having a reaction**.

**Prevention Strategies Our App Should Educate Users About:**

1. **Preparation Order**
   - Prepare allergen-free food first
   - Avoid cooking items side-by-side (risk of splattering)
   - Don't pick allergens out of food (proteins remain)

2. **Equipment and Surfaces**
   - Clean all surfaces with hot, soapy water before and after
   - Designate specific utensils and cutting boards for allergen-free cooking
   - Label and store designated equipment separately
   - Hand sanitizer is NOT sufficient - must use soap and water

3. **Storage**
   - Place allergen-containing foods on lower shelves
   - Store allergen-free foods above to prevent dripping
   - Individually wrap and seal foods
   - Use separate containers

4. **Cooking Methods**
   - Don't share fryers, grills, or baking sheets
   - Use fresh oil for allergen-free frying
   - Cover allergen-free foods when cooking nearby allergens
   - Be aware of airborne proteins (shellfish, fish steam)

5. **Condiments and Shared Items**
   - Use squeeze bottles to prevent cross-contact
   - Avoid "double-dipping" utensils
   - Keep separate jars of common condiments if needed

6. **Cleaning**
   - Sponges and towels can harbor allergens - use disposable when possible
   - Change dishcloths frequently
   - Run dishwasher on hot cycle
   - Clean can openers and other overlooked items
